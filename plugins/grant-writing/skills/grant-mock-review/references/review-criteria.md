# Peer Review Criteria and Panel Behavior by Funder

Verify against the current FOA/solicitation — criteria and weighting are mechanism-specific. Summaries below reflect guidance current as of mid-2026 (NIH simplified review framework effective Jan 2025; NSF PAPPG 24-1 with Supplements NSF 26-200/26-202).

## NIH — Simplified Review Framework

Most research project grants (R01, R21, etc.) are scored on three factors:

- **Factor 1 — Importance of the Research (Significance & Innovation)**: scored 1–9. Are the aims important; will success change concepts, methods, or practice; is the work innovative in a way that matters.
- **Factor 2 — Rigor and Feasibility (Approach)**: scored 1–9. Rigor of design and methods; feasibility given preliminary data, timeline, and resources; relevant biological variables; statistical rigor.
- **Factor 3 — Expertise and Resources**: rated **sufficient** or **gaps identified** — no numeric score. A named gap still depresses Overall Impact.
- **Overall Impact (1–9, 1 = best)**: integrates all three and drives percentiling and funding. It is a judgment, not an average — reviewers are told to weigh, and in practice a single major Factor 2 weakness caps the score regardless of significance.
- **Additional review considerations** (human subjects, inclusion, vertebrate animals, biohazards, budget/period, resource sharing/DMSP, authentication): rated acceptable/unacceptable, not folded into the score, but an unacceptable rating bars award until resolved.

### Score calibration

| Score | Descriptor | Weakness profile |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Exceptional | essentially none |
| 2 | Outstanding | negligible |
| 3 | Excellent | minor weaknesses only |
| 4 | Very Good | at least one moderate weakness |
| 5 | Good | moderate weaknesses |
| 6 | Satisfactory | several moderate weaknesses |
| 7 | Fair | at least one major weakness |
| 8 | Marginal | a few major weaknesses |
| 9 | Poor | numerous major weaknesses |

The adjectives mislead: a "Good" (5) is not fundable for investigator-initiated R01s in a typical cycle. A *moderate* weakness lessens impact; a *major* weakness severely limits it. Pick the number from the weakness taxonomy, not the flattering word.

### How the panel actually behaves

- Three assigned reviewers submit written critiques and preliminary scores before the meeting. Roughly the lower-scoring half is triaged (**Not Discussed**): the PI gets the written critiques but no discussion, resume, or final impact score.
- A discussed application gets on the order of 10–15 minutes. Reviewer 1 frames it and states a score; reviewers 2 and 3 add or dissent; the stated scores set the range and the full panel then votes privately, with members voting outside the range required to declare it. Final scores regress toward the assigned reviewers' anchor — which is why the two assigned-reviewer personas matter most in simulation.
- Discussion concentrates on disagreements between assigned reviewers and on whichever weakness reviewer 1 leads with. Enthusiastic champions rescue borderline applications; an application with no champion drifts down even without a fatal flaw.
- The summary statement is the (largely unedited) critiques plus, for discussed applications, a resume of discussion — the resume is the panel's collective judgment and the highest-signal text in the document.

## NSF — Two Merit Review Criteria (PAPPG 24-1)

- **Intellectual Merit**: potential to advance knowledge.
- **Broader Impacts**: potential to benefit society / achieve desired societal outcomes.

Both assessed through five elements: (1) potential to advance knowledge / benefit society; (2) creative, original, potentially transformative concepts; (3) well-reasoned, well-organized plan with a mechanism to assess success; (4) qualifications of team/organization; (5) adequacy of resources. Reviewers give strengths/weaknesses per criterion and an overall rating (Excellent / Very Good / Good / Fair / Poor). Solicitation-specific criteria may be added — check.

Panel behavior: ratings skew high, so a plain "Good" usually signals a proposal in trouble. Panels typically produce a consensus panel summary and place proposals in recommendation categories (naming varies by division — e.g., High/Medium/Low Priority, or Fund/Do Not Fund). NSF program officers exercise substantially more discretion relative to panel rank than NIH program staff do relative to percentile. Recurring score-killers: Broader Impacts that are generic or bolted-on rather than integrated with the research, and project plans that list activities with no way to assess success (element 3).

## DoD — varies by agency/program

- **CDMRP**: two-tier — peer review (scientific merit) then programmatic review (portfolio/relevance fit); criteria published per program announcement, typically impact, innovation, approach, and the stated military/disease relevance. Many mechanisms seat consumer/advocate reviewers who score in part from the lay abstract — a weak lay abstract is a scoring event, not a formality.
- **DARPA / service labs (ONR, ARO, AFOSR)**: evaluated per the BAA's stated factors, in the stated order of importance. The **Heilmeier Catechism** is the informal spine: What are you trying to do (no jargon)? How is it done today, and what are the limits? What's new and why will it succeed? Who cares? If successful, what difference does it make? What are the risks? Cost and duration? What are the mid-term and final "exams"?

## Foundations

Criteria vary widely; extract them from the RFP. Common axes: mission alignment, significance to the funder's focus population, feasibility, investigator fit, and (frequently) lay-summary clarity, since board members are often non-scientists. Board review may weight non-scientific factors (institutional or geographic distribution) the RFP discloses.
