# Peer Review Criteria by Funder

Verify against the current FOA/solicitation — criteria and weighting are mechanism-specific. Summaries below reflect guidance current as of mid-2026.

## NIH — Simplified Review Framework (due dates on/after Jan 25, 2025)

Most research project grants (R01, R21, etc.) are scored on three factors:

- **Factor 1 — Importance of the Research (Significance & Innovation)**: scored 1–9. Are the aims important; will success change concepts/methods/practice; is the work innovative in a way that matters.
- **Factor 2 — Rigor and Feasibility (Approach)**: scored 1–9. Scientific rigor of design and methods; feasibility given preliminary data, timeline, resources; consideration of relevant biological variables; statistical rigor.
- **Factor 3 — Expertise and Resources**: evaluated as sufficient or with identified gaps (not a 1–9 score). Are the investigators and environment appropriate for the work as proposed.

**Overall Impact score (1–9, 1 = best)** integrates all three; it is the score that drives funding decisions and percentiling. Additional review considerations (not scored into impact but can affect it): protection of human subjects, inclusion, vertebrate animals, biohazards, budget and period, resource sharing, authentication.

Scores: 1–3 high impact, 4–6 moderate, 7–9 low. Roughly the top-scoring half of applications are discussed; the rest are "Not Discussed" (ND).

## NSF — Two Merit Review Criteria (PAPPG 24-1)

- **Intellectual Merit**: potential to advance knowledge.
- **Broader Impacts**: potential to benefit society / achieve desired societal outcomes.

Both assessed through five elements: (1) potential to advance knowledge / benefit society; (2) creative, original, potentially transformative concepts; (3) well-reasoned, well-organized plan with rationale for success; (4) qualifications of team/organization; (5) adequacy of resources. Reviewers give strengths/weaknesses per criterion and an overall rating (Excellent / Very Good / Good / Fair / Poor) with a recommendation. Solicitation-specific review criteria may be added.

## DoD — varies by agency/program

- **CDMRP**: peer review (scientific merit) then programmatic review (portfolio/relevance fit); criteria published per program announcement, typically impact, innovation, approach, and specified military/disease relevance. Many mechanisms use consumer/advocate reviewers who weight the lay abstract.
- **DARPA / service labs (ONR, ARO, AFOSR)**: evaluated per the BAA's stated factors. The **Heilmeier Catechism** is the informal spine: What are you trying to do (no jargon)? How is it done today, and what are the limits? What's new in your approach and why will it succeed? Who cares? If successful, what difference does it make? What are the risks? How much will it cost, how long? What are the mid-term and final "exams" (success metrics)?

## Foundations
Criteria vary widely; extract them from the RFP. Common axes: mission alignment, significance to the funder's focus population, feasibility, investigator fit, and (frequently) the lay/public summary's clarity. Board review may weight non-scientific factors (institutional diversity, geographic distribution) the RFP discloses.
